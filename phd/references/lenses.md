# Research Lenses

Use lenses to structure a topic from multiple angles. A lens is not an output by itself. It shapes what questions to ask.

## Spatial / Modular Lens

Use to understand parts, modules, layers, interfaces, and boundaries.

Ask:
- What are the components?
- How are components connected?
- Where are the interfaces and bottlenecks?
- What is inside or outside the system boundary?

Output:
- module map
- architecture table
- boundary notes

## Temporal / Stage Lens

Use to understand change over time.

Ask:
- What are the phases?
- What triggered each transition?
- What became possible or obsolete at each stage?
- What remained stable?

Output:
- timeline
- phase table
- transition causes

## Causal / Mechanistic Lens

Use to explain why something happens.

Ask:
- What variables matter?
- What mechanisms connect causes to effects?
- What feedback loops exist?
- What are plausible confounders?

Output:
- causal chain
- mechanism map
- variable table

## Historical / Genealogical Lens

Use to trace origins, intellectual lineages, and inherited assumptions.

Ask:
- Where did the idea or practice come from?
- Which earlier problems shaped it?
- Which people, organizations, or artifacts influenced it?
- What assumptions were inherited?

Output:
- genealogy map
- history brief
- lineage table

## Actor / Organization Lens

Use when people, labs, companies, communities, or institutions matter.

Ask:
- Who are the relevant actors?
- What did each contribute?
- What incentives and constraints shape them?
- How do they relate to each other?

Output:
- actor map
- organization table
- contribution matrix

## Method / Technical Route Lens

Use to compare ways of solving a problem.

Ask:
- What methods exist?
- What assumptions does each method make?
- What resources or capabilities does each require?
- Where does each fail?

Output:
- method taxonomy
- technical route comparison
- tradeoff table

## Problem-Solution Lens

Use to connect needs, constraints, and proposed solutions.

Ask:
- What problem is being solved?
- Who has the problem?
- What counts as success?
- Which solutions exist and what do they trade off?

Output:
- problem tree
- solution map
- tradeoff memo

## Economic / Incentive Lens

Use when adoption, funding, markets, labor, or institutional behavior matter.

Ask:
- Who pays?
- Who benefits?
- Who bears the cost or risk?
- What incentives explain behavior?

Output:
- incentive map
- stakeholder table
- adoption constraints

## Governance / Power Lens

Use when control, standards, regulation, coordination, or authority matter.

Ask:
- Who can decide?
- Who can block?
- What rules or norms matter?
- What power asymmetries exist?

Output:
- governance map
- power analysis
- control points

## Adoption / Diffusion Lens

Use to understand how an idea, technology, or practice spreads.

Ask:
- Who adopts first?
- What reduces friction?
- What creates resistance?
- Which complements must exist?

Output:
- adoption pathway
- diffusion stages
- barrier list

## Failure Mode Lens

Use to stress-test a framework, proposal, or hypothesis.

Ask:
- How could this fail?
- Which assumptions are brittle?
- What edge cases matter?
- What evidence would overturn the view?

Output:
- failure mode table
- risk register
- falsification notes
